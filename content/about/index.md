# About Me

My name is Alex Verboon

My journey into technology started around 1987 during a computer training class at school. The systems were IBM PS/2 computers running MS-DOS, and one of the first things I learned was how to navigate the command line and use basic DOS commands. While the lessons were simple by today's standards, they sparked a curiosity about technology and how computers worked behind the scenes.

The real turning point came in 1990 when I started my apprenticeship in the forwarding department of ABB Power Generation. The workplace used IBM mainframe terminals alongside IBM PS/2 desktop computers. While many colleagues saw computers simply as tools to complete their daily tasks, I quickly discovered they could do much more.

One of my earliest lessons in automation came when processing shipping orders on a mainframe application. Rather than entering the same information repeatedly, I figured out how to create a macro that automatically filled in parts of the process for me. It was a simple improvement, but it taught me an important lesson that has stayed with me throughout my career: technology should eliminate repetitive work and help people focus on more meaningful tasks.

Around the same time, the department relied on a logistics application developed by an external software company. Fascinated by the fact that someone could create such an application, I started teaching myself Clipper programming. What began as curiosity quickly turned into action. When the department needed a solution to manage transport logistics quotations, I volunteered to build it. The result was one of my first business applications, followed shortly by a time-tracking solution used to allocate project costs across departments.

These early projects taught me two important lessons: technology is most valuable when it solves real business problems, and some of the best opportunities come from saying "yes" before you fully know how you are going to solve the challenge.

As PCs became more common throughout the organization, I gradually became the unofficial IT resource within the department. I installed PCs, deployed applications, fixed printer issues, managed the Novell network, supported Lotus Notes, and eventually became responsible for Oracle databases. Without realizing it at the time, I was building a broad foundation across applications, infrastructure, databases, and end-user support that would prove invaluable later in my career.

## Moving into Enterprise IT

In 1995, ABB Power Generation launched a major transformation initiative called Operation 95. The goal was to centralize IT services across the organization. Until then, each department largely managed its own infrastructure, servers, and support.

I was fortunate to be invited to join the newly established central IT team. This marked my transition from departmental IT into enterprise IT. My responsibilities included developing a CMDB application to track IT assets, supporting the rollout of the corporate Windows desktop environment, and providing Oracle database services to other departments.

Looking back, this was probably the first time I unknowingly moved from supporting technology to delivering an IT service.

When ABB later decided to outsource its IT operations to Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC), I joined Digital and entered the world of professional IT services. Not long after, Digital became part of Compaq, and later HP. These years exposed me to large-scale enterprise computing and some of the most significant technology shifts of the late 1990s and early 2000s.

As Windows 2000 and Active Directory emerged, I became part of a Technology Innovation Team responsible for creating blueprints and reference architectures for future outsourcing customers. Together with the team, we designed and implemented Windows 2000 client and server solutions, Active Directory architectures, Group Policy standards, automated deployment processes, and application packaging methodologies.

What made this work particularly rewarding was seeing these solutions move from the lab into production environments supporting real customers.

## Building Global Services

As Compaq evolved into HP, our work gained visibility beyond Switzerland. One milestone was supporting a major global outsourcing initiative for a well-known automotive manufacturer. During the presales phase, our team helped define the technical solution and service model. After the deal was won, we also became involved in the implementation.

Around the same time, I was given the opportunity to establish and lead the EMEA competency center for Workstation Image Management Services.

Long before Microsoft's Business Desktop Deployment and Microsoft Deployment Toolkit became industry standards, our team had already built highly automated deployment and image management solutions using Altiris. We leveraged PXE-based deployment, automated image creation, application packaging, hardware abstraction, and centralized lifecycle management to deliver a repeatable service at scale.

From approximately 2000 until 2012, I served as lead engineer and service delivery manager for this service. Over the years, more than 50 local, regional, and global organizations adopted our standardized desktop image services. We collaborated closely with teams in Europe, Australia, and Canada to provide global coverage and support.

This experience reinforced several principles that continue to influence my work today: automation enables scale, standardization enables quality, and successful services require a balance between engineering excellence and operational execution.

## Returning to Engineering

After more than a decade at HP, I felt it was time for a new challenge. While the work had been rewarding, I increasingly found myself drawn back to engineering. Although management responsibilities had become a significant part of my role, I still considered myself an engineer at heart.

In 2012, I joined Swiss Re as an engineer. For me, it was not a step backwards but a return to what had always motivated me most: technology.

Initially, I focused on Microsoft Configuration Manager, PowerShell, Windows client management, operating system deployment, and Group Policy. However, it did not take long before I became involved in larger transformation initiatives. During a workplace technology reorganization, I was asked to lead the Workplace Product Integration team.

The team's mission was to evaluate, plan, and implement the next generation of workplace technologies. This included major transformation programs such as Windows 10 adoption, Skype for Business, Exchange modernization, and a complete redesign of an aging Citrix environment.

## Discovering Cybersecurity

Looking back, security had always been part of my work, even if I did not consider myself a security professional. Keeping systems updated, deploying security patches, hardening endpoints through Group Policy, and reducing risk through standardization were all part of daily life.

The real turning point came during the planning and rollout of Windows 10. The CISO approached me with an exciting challenge. Microsoft was preparing to launch Windows Defender Advanced Threat Protection, known today as Microsoft Defender for Endpoint. At the time, the product was still in private preview and represented a completely new approach to endpoint protection and detection.

I was tasked with integrating Defender ATP into the new Windows 10 client platform and helping prepare the organization for its adoption.

Around the same time, I was given the opportunity to attend a SANS training and certification program. Combined with the Defender ATP implementation, this significantly deepened my interest in cybersecurity and marked the beginning of a new chapter in my career.

Microsoft also introduced me to another technology that would have a lasting impact on my professional journey: Kusto Query Language (KQL).

Having spent years advocating for automation through PowerShell, I immediately recognized the potential of KQL. I started experimenting with it, applying it to real-world scenarios, and gradually integrating it into my daily work. What started as curiosity quickly became a passion.

## Sharing Knowledge and Building Community

Sharing knowledge has always been an important part of my professional journey. I started blogging in 2008 to document lessons learned and help others solve similar challenges.

One of the first moments I realized the blog was reaching people beyond my immediate network came when I joined Swiss Re. Although I had never worked with my new teammates before, several of them already knew who I was because they had been reading my blog.

As my focus shifted toward cybersecurity, I continued sharing knowledge through blog posts, GitHub repositories, presentations, and community contributions. Around 2018, Microsoft invited me to speak about Microsoft Defender from a customer perspective. In 2019, I was honored to receive the Microsoft MVP award in the Security category, recognizing my contributions to the community.

Community involvement has always been important to me. Since its founding, I have been a member of the Workplace Ninja Summit organizing team. What started as a community-driven event has grown into one of Europe's leading Microsoft-focused conferences, bringing together professionals from around the world to learn, share experiences, and build connections.

In 2022, together with my friend Gianni Castaldi, I launched KQL Cafe, a community initiative dedicated to Kusto Query Language and the Microsoft security ecosystem. Through interviews, demonstrations, and technical discussions, KQL Cafe has helped connect KQL practitioners and security professionals around the world.

Building on the success of KQL Cafe and the growing Kusto community, Gianni and I launched KustoCon in 2024. The conference provides a dedicated platform for professionals working with KQL, Microsoft Sentinel, Microsoft Defender, Azure Data Explorer, Microsoft Fabric, and other technologies built on the Kusto platform.

## Today

Today, I work at baseVISION as CTO and Cyber Security Consultant.

As CTO, my focus is to ensure that baseVISION remains at the forefront of technology, particularly within cybersecurity. A significant part of my role involves understanding customer challenges, monitoring industry trends, evaluating emerging technologies, and translating those insights into services that help organizations strengthen their security posture and achieve their business objectives.

The emergence of AI represents another major technology shift, similar to the transitions from mainframes to PCs, from client-server to cloud, and from traditional IT to modern cybersecurity that I have witnessed throughout my career. It is an exciting time to be working in technology, and I believe we are only at the beginning of what AI will enable.

What continues to motivate me is the opportunity to learn, experiment, share knowledge, and help others succeed. After nearly four decades in IT, I remain as curious about technology as I was during that first computer training class on an IBM PS/2 running MS-DOS.

**If there is one principle that has guided my career, it is this: never stop learning, and never stop sharing what you learn with others.**
